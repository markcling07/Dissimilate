Three days inside a forest that never once stopped dripping.

day one

Trailhead to Camp Enting

Into the range

The Mt. Balatukan Range Natural Park signboard stands where the road above Gingoog finally gives up, and past it the trail wastes no time going uphill. We signed in, posed while our legs were still fresh, and started the long approach through sunlit second-growth — the easy, deceptive part of any big mountain. By afternoon we reached the stopover at Camp Rosas Pandan, where a sign helpfully announced 1.86 kilometers before Camp Enting. Nobody tells you those are vertical kilometers. We made Enting by dusk, pitched under the first real moss, and slept to the sound of the forest wringing itself out.

day two

The Cloud Forest

The mossy heart

Above Enting the mountain changes its language entirely. Every trunk, every branch, every fallen log wears a green coat a hand-span thick, and the trail becomes a rumor you follow through a dripping cathedral. We climbed over root ladders, ducked under moss curtains, and drank our coffee walking because there was nowhere dry to sit. It is the kind of forest that makes you lower your voice without agreeing to — older than the trail, older than the town below it, and completely indifferent to your itinerary.

The moss doesn't cover this mountain. It is the mountain.

The cold up here is honest — the red jacket came out before breakfast and stayed on. Between the giants the forest kept handing us small things to look at: bracket fungi stacked like shelf lanterns, orchids freeloading on every limb, a canopy you could climb into and sit in like furniture. We did.

Camp Enting · Golden Hour

The forest kept us all day. The sky paid us back at six.

day three

Summit & Descent

The roof of Misamis

We left camp in the dark and traded the moss for wind on the last push to the summit — 2,450 meters, one of the highest stands of ground in Mindanao, with the clouds arranged politely below us. Golden hour up there is a slow, extravagant thing; we let it run long, found a bough to recline on like it was upholstered, and put off the descent as far as decency allowed. Then there was nothing left to do but go down, which is the only sad part of any mountain.
