The Ascent: Trails & Twin Echoes

We began our climb at the foothills of Tupi, South Cotabato, where Mount Matutum stands as an imposing, symmetrical volcanic cone rising 2,286 meters above sea level. Starting early, the air was crisp, and we walked through the expansive pineapple plantations that cover the lower slopes before entering the dense forest canopy.

We enjoyed our hike through the Dole pineapple fields, though unfortunately, we weren't able to get a photo of the landscape with Mount Matutum in the background.

As a group, our strides were naturally synchronized at the start, but Matutum's terrain quickly challenged our rhythm. The lower trail is a steep ascent, with clay paths made slick by morning dew. The physical exertion began immediately, testing our collective endurance as we transitioned from the open farmland into the cooler, shadow-draped secondary forest.

What struck me was the sheer verticality of the climb. We weren't just hiking; in many places, we were scrambling on all fours, pulling ourselves up by grasping massive tree roots that seemed to form natural ladders along the ridges.

The Sanctuary: Canopy of the Sacred

As we climbed past 1,500 meters, we entered the mossy forest zone—a critical sanctuary inside Matutum's 15,600-hectare protected landscape. The temperature dropped sharply, and the tropical forest transformed into a silent, cloud-drenched dreamscape of stunted trees draped in thick layers of green moss.

The mossy forest behaves like a giant sponge, trapping water from the clouds and feeding the major river systems of South Cotabato. You can feel the water cycle in action—the mist clinging to your skin, the moisture dripping from every branch.

It makes you realize that the mountain isn't just a climbing destination. It is an ecological life support system for the entire region. Preserving these mossy heights is what keeps the agricultural valleys below fertile and alive.

The Summit: Crest of the Dormant Giant

Lera: The final stretch to the summit is the most challenging. At 2,286 MASL, the views of South Cotabato are breathtaking, but looking inward is even more striking—a deep, forested volcanic basin.

Lara: Matutum is a dormant stratovolcano, and its last recorded activity was a suspected phreatic eruption in 1911. So, it's not gonna explode, we're all safe.

Lera: Holding the wooden summit marker, the sense of achievement was overwhelming. The cloud cover would part for just a few seconds, revealing the vast agricultural landscapes below before swallowing us back into the mist.

Lara: It was the perfect culmination of our journey. We shared a quiet moment looking out over the clouds, realizing that the summit is not just a high point, but a vantage point to appreciate the fragile beauty of this ancient volcano.

2,286 METERS ABOVE THE SEA

Epilogue: Looking Out

Lera: Standing at the summit marker, the cold wind whipped our hair and the clouds swirled like a turbulent ocean around us. We looked out at the vast expanse of South Cotabato, feeling the sheer scale of the peak we had just conquered.

Lara: In that quiet moment, we stood side by side. We had climbed as individuals, testing our own physical limits against the volcano, but we stood at the peak as one—two sisters sharing the quiet triumph of the summit.
