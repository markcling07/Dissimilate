Buko-Buko Peak

A climb by Kim

One morning, a bamboo walking stick, and a whole ridge of grass and sky above General Santos — the kind of day that stays quiet and green in your memory.

A little travel log by Kim

Starting in the Green

The trail up Buko-Buko begins low and leafy — banana leaves overhead, coffee and forest closing in on both sides, the ground still cool and soft from the night. Everyone grabbed a bamboo stick at the trailhead, and off we went, single file, laughing our way into the trees.

Then the mud. The path had that early-morning slip to it, so the sticks earned their keep quickly and the pace slowed to something honest.

Then the Ridge Opens Up

And just like that, the forest lets go. The trees drop away and the whole world becomes grass and sky — rolling emerald ridges folding into each other, and far across the valley, Mt. Matutum standing quiet above the clouds. This is the part nobody warns you about: how suddenly it gets beautiful.

"You climb through the trees not knowing — and then the grass opens, and there's the whole sky waiting."

A Sea of Clouds

By the time we were high on the ridge, the clouds had come up to meet us — spilling over the far slopes and pooling in the valleys like a slow white tide. One moment the mountains were sharp and green, the next they were half-dissolved in mist.

We just stood there for a while. Some views you don't photograph so much as witness.

Just Grass and Sky

The upper trail is all open grassland — tall cogon brushing your arms, the ridge line curving up ahead, and hardly anything between you and the clouds. It's steep in places, and your legs remember it later, but it never stops being gentle to look at.

Buko-Buko Peak

And then the sign — hand-painted green letters, a Philippine flag snapping in the wind, and the whole of General Santos spread out below. Buko-Buko Peak, Barangay Upper Labay. We made it. Muddy shoes, happy faces, and a summit all to ourselves.

No grand reason for this climb, no big lesson waiting at the top — just a morning of good friends, wet grass, and a mountain generous with its views. Sometimes that's the whole point. Buko-Buko gave us the trees, the mud, the clouds, and the sky, and asked for nothing back but the walk.

— Kim
