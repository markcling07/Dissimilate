A photo essay by Raul

Sumagaya

One mountain. Two climbs. Twenty-eight years apart.

Part One

1996

We were young. In October 1996 we walked into Sumagaya's mossy forest with film cameras, borrowed packs, and no idea the photographs would one day matter this much.

February 2, 1998

Cebu Pacific Flight 387, flying Manila to Cagayan de Oro,
crashed into the slopes of Mt. Sumagaya.

All 104 aboard were lost.

The newspapers wrote Mt. Sumagaya. So did the Air Force's 505th rescue team — it was the name their maps gave the place they were looking at. But the slope that caught the plane belongs to Mt. Lumot, Sumagaya's neighbor on the same ridge. It is part of why the site was so hard to find: for days the searchers worked from a name that was not quite the place, until mountaineers were asked to come and lead them in. The record kept the wrong mountain. The right one kept everything else.

Two years after our photographs, the mountain became a memorial.

Part Two

The Return

Decades later, I went back. The trail is steeper when you are older — but the forest was exactly as I left it. The moss remembers nothing. Or maybe it remembers everything.

Part Three

What the Mountain Keeps

Then, scattered in the ferns — metal. Landing gear. Fuselage. Twenty-seven years on, the wreckage of Flight 387 is still there, slowly being folded into the forest floor.

I climbed Sumagaya twice. The first time, it was just a mountain.
The second time, I understood it was keeping something for people who never came home.

— Raul
