# You don't need to fire both

**TL;DR:** There's a popular interview question — "Who would you fire, X or Y?" As a long-time employee of collective companies, my answer is: no one. If two people teamed up and finished the task according to your expectations, there is nothing to punish. The question says more about the person asking it than about the people it's asked about.

## Who would you fire — X or Y?

None. That's the whole answer.

When I see a video, or hear someone in a training room ask "Who would you fire, the X or the Y?", I already know what answer they're fishing for. They want you to weigh two workers against each other and pick a loser. They want to see if you can be *decisive*.

But look at the actual situation: they already teamed up to finish the task according to your expectations. The work got done. The standard was met. Where exactly is the firing offense?

> The task was finished. The expectation was met. What are we punishing?

## What is the question really testing?

It's testing whether you think like a manager who needs a villain. The question assumes that in every pair, someone must be the problem — that credit is a pie and blame is mandatory.

That's not management. That's bookkeeping for egos.

A team that quietly splits the work — one carries the skill, the other carries the grind, however they arranged it — solved *your* staffing problem for free. They self-organized. Companies pay consultants to make that happen.

## What happens when you fire one anyway?

You teach everyone the real lesson: cooperation is dangerous. Help your seatmate and you might become the "weaker one" in someone's hypothetical. Next time, nobody teams up. Everybody protects their own numbers, documents their own contribution, and lets the task suffer — because the task was never what you were measuring anyway.

If the output met your expectations, the pair worked. You don't need to fire both. You don't need to fire either. You need to ask yourself why your first instinct, when shown two people who succeeded together, was to reach for the axe.
