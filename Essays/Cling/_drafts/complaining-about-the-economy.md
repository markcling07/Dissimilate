# Complaining about the Economy

**TL;DR:** Before you complain that this economy sucks, you have to be taxed first. Complaints carry weight when they come from inside the game — from people working, paying, and carrying the load. That doesn't mean every complaint is invalid: prices that doubled "temporarily" and never came back down deserve all the noise we can make. But make your noise as a player, not from the lobby.

## Who gets to complain about the economy?

The people who are in it. Before you complain that this economy sucks, you have to be taxed first. You can't just say something about a certain thing you are not part of.

Instead of going on the streets, raising your voice, why not fill out an application form? Then, when you see your tax deducted on your payslip, that's the time you complain. You'll even complain better — you'll have numbers.

> Don't stay at the lobby. Join the game.

Going against the government because you thought that shouting is participation — it isn't. Participation is employment, taxes, and receipts. Anger without a stake in the outcome is just noise with a schedule.

And think practically for a second. Imagine, on your job interview, the recruiter saw you on Facebook doing stuff like this — burning effigies, shouting into a megaphone about jobs you've never applied for. Unemployable. You became a full-time critic of a game you never entered.

## Are all complaints invalid then?

No — some complaints are completely valid, and here's mine.

This damn country has been through one hell of a tragedy, more than once. Even a slight global event can cause massive changes in the Philippines, and things often never return to normal. The prices go up like an elevator and come down like a staircase — a staircase that's usually out of order.

- During the COVID-19 pandemic, because social distancing was required, fares were doubled. Understandable. But after COVID? They're still double.
- Even before the war with Iran, if oil companies decided to increase prices by ₱5 or ₱10 for some reason, those prices would not go down easily — sometimes not at all — even after everything was resolved.

This is a valid complaint, by the way. These issues need to be addressed right away once everything is normal. If the situation that caused the changes has been resolved, those changes should also be reversed. A temporary emergency should not have a permanent price tag.

## What's the difference between the two kinds of complainers?

One pays for the problem; the other performs about it. The worker complaining about ratchet prices is describing a wound on their own payslip. The lobby complainer is describing a headline.

To the performers: you can't represent us in our struggle. You see, we are already thinking about how to make ends meet — and then we have to think about you too. We are so occupied with making ends meet. Don't give us another thing to think of.

Get in the game. Get taxed. Then let's complain together — properly, loudly, and with payslips as evidence.
