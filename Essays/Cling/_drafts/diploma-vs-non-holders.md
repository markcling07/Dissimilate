# Diploma vs non-holders

**TL;DR:** Every day, TikTok and Facebook tell you a college degree is useless — start a side hustle instead. That advice always comes from people who already have the diploma. I'm 29, with 7 years of BPO experience and no degree, and I can tell you what the videos leave out: the problem was never whether the work is hard. The problem is the doors that stay locked no matter how good you are.

## What do degree holders say about the job market?

Mostly, that the degree wasn't worth it — and they say it from inside the building. My friends who finished college say the job market doesn't actually require graduates because the jobs are easy. They regret going to college when they could have done side hustles or a business that (they think) would eventually make them rich — influenced by cheap content on Facebook and TikTok about how degrees are useless nowadays.

Most of my workmates in the BPO who finished college complain that the market offers low salaries for their degrees, so they ended up in the call center, where the pay is higher than the jobs that fit their course.

Notice something? Both groups are complaining about doors they were able to open.

## What does the job market look like without a diploma?

Like a wall you can't see until you hit it. For me — 29 years old, 7 years of BPO experience — the work has never been the problem. In this industry, I can be promoted if I perform well. Except most positions, even the very next step up from agent, somehow require a diploma.

You guys have no idea how hard it is not having a degree. You will never get the feeling that most doors cannot be opened for us.

### The luxury of calling a job "easy"

It's easy to look at an entry-level position and call the work "simple" when you already have the credentials to sit in the chair. For those of us without a diploma, the difficulty isn't the work itself — it's the gatekeeping. The job market isn't a meritocracy based on what you *can* do; it's an automated screening process. Your resume doesn't get rejected by a human who thinks you aren't smart enough. It gets filtered out by software before a human ever sees it.

### The glass ceiling of promotion

Even in industries that call themselves merit-based, the ceiling is real. You can outperform everyone, log the most hours, and keep a flawless track record for seven, eight, nine years. Then you apply for the next tier, and the form has a mandatory checkbox: *College Graduate.*

> Without that piece of paper, you aren't fighting the other candidates. You're fighting company policy.

### The doors you don't even see

Degree holders complain about low entry salaries — and that frustration is valid. But they rarely acknowledge the baseline security the diploma gives them. A diploma is a universal key. It might only open the door to a low-paying job at first, but it *opens the door*. Without it, you live with the exhausting knowledge that entire sectors of the economy are permanently locked away from you — regardless of your IQ, your work ethic, or your experience.

## So is the "degrees are useless" crowd right?

No — they're selling something. Usually views, sometimes a course. The job market *is* broken, but not because degrees are useless. It's broken because it still values a piece of paper over years of proven, real-world competence.

Until hiring catches up to reality, pretending the barrier doesn't exist doesn't help anyone. It just minimizes the hustle of those of us trying to break through it — from the outside, where the checkbox is.
