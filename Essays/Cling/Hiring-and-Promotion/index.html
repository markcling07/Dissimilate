Hiring & Promotion: Alignment over Qualification

By Cling · July 19, 2026

TL;DR Just because you are qualified, it doesn’t mean you will be hired or promoted. Even if you check all the boxes, you may still fall behind a certain candidate — because employers and management often don’t need raw qualifications. They need alignment.

If the corporate world solely cared about efficiency, the most qualified candidate would always win. Unfortunately, it doesn’t work that way. When you become a manager, your instinct shifts toward protecting your inner circle, your existing structures, and your established workarounds.

This reality forced me to realize a hard truth: under the current system, assuming I have what it takes, I cannot be promoted. Here is why.

1. The Threat of the High-Performer

Let’s look at a common workplace scenario. You are an established manager in a company. Your team and the neighboring teams are doing just fine, and executive leadership assumes the operational ceiling has been reached.

Then, a new manager is hired. He is the “best candidate” — deeply experienced and highly efficient. Suddenly, this new guy starts crushing KPIs and exceeding expectations across all aspects of the job. Executive leadership sees his potential and immediately puts him on the fast track for a promotion.

But how does that make you feel? As a manager who has spent years trying to maintain a comfortable, “chill” job, that high-performer isn’t a teammate — he is a threat who just raised the ceiling for everyone else.

2. The Invisible Behavioral Filter

Because of this friction, corporate survival kicks in. If you are the person trying to disrupt the status quo, the existing management structure will notice. In fact, you are being observed right from the start.

The moment management hears that a disruptive high-performer is trying to apply for a promotion, they have often already rejected them in their minds. This rejection has everything to do with behavioral alignment. If you don’t “behave” or act like a traditional manager — which often means protecting the peace rather than rocking the boat — you are filtered out before the interview even begins.

3. Departmental Monopoly

The final barrier to pure meritocracy is corporate feudalism, or monopoly. Imagine a company with five teams, and I am the boss of Team A. To secure my own political power, I will actively ensure that my team members are the ones promoted into neighboring managerial roles. If my co-managers used to work under me, I successfully maintain control over the entire department and secure my structure until I get promoted myself.

Because promotion in this environment is strictly controlled, delegated, and vouched for, the game is rigged against outsiders.

If you don’t have that specific tribal connection, it doesn’t matter how good you are — you will never get the position.
